How to choose the right white noise machine

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best white noise machine for you depends on your sleep environment, budget, and desired sound variety, look for adjustable volume, a broad range of sounds, and a reliable build.

Why use a white noise machine?

White noise machines fill your room with a steady, broadband sound that masks sudden noises from traffic, neighbours, or a partner’s snoring. That masking effect helps you fall asleep faster and stay asleep longer, especially in noisy or unpredictable environments. Beyond sleep, many people use them for focus while working, to calm anxious pets, or to soothe babies. A consistent acoustic backdrop reduces the brain’s startle response to abrupt sounds, which is why they’re popular in nurseries and open-plan offices alike. Not all machines are created equal, though. The most effective ones produce a truly random, static-like noise (often called “true white noise”) rather than a repeating loop or fan-like rumble. True white noise covers a wide frequency range, making it more effective at hiding other sounds.

What features matter most?

Start with sound quality and variety. Look for a machine that offers at least a few distinct sound profiles: white noise, pink noise (deeper, more bass-heavy), and a handful of nature sounds like rain or ocean. The best models let you adjust the tone (bass/treble) as well as the volume, so you can fine-tune the mask to your room acoustics. Volume range is critical. Most machines have a gentle lowest setting and a fairly loud maximum, but you want one with enough granularity in between. A machine that only has three volume levels is far less useful than one with a continuous dial or at least eight steps. Also consider timer and memory functions. A timer lets the unit run for 30, 60, or 90 minutes and then shut off automatically, good if you prefer silence once you’re deeply asleep. A memory feature saves your last volume and sound setting so you don’t have to reset every time you turn it on.

Sound quality: real white noise or fan-like noise?

True white noise is a random hiss that contains all audible frequencies at equal intensity. It sounds like an untuned radio static. Many dedicated machines generate this electronically, while others use a fan to create a rushing-air sound. Fan-based machines are often cheaper and can produce a pleasant “whoosh,” but the frequency balance is narrower and may not mask a wide range of noises. For sleep masking, true digital white noise is generally more effective, especially for mid- to high-frequency sounds like voices or sirens. Pink noise, which has more low-frequency energy, can be more soothing for some people and is sometimes recommended for deep sleep enhancement. If you’re sensitive to high-pitched static, test a machine’s sound profile before buying, or choose one with multiple sound options so you can switch to a deeper “brown noise” or a rain track.

Portable vs. plug-in: which form factor fits your life?

Portable, battery-operated machines are great for travel, naps on the go, or homes where outlets are awkward. They tend to be smaller and lighter, but may have smaller speakers and less volume range. Many can also run on USB power, which expands their usefulness. Plug-in machines are generally more powerful, produce fuller sound, and can run indefinitely without worrying about battery life. They often have larger speakers and better tone controls. If your primary use is at home, especially in a bedroom, a plug-in model is usually the better investment. There are also combination models that plug in but include a rechargeable battery for short-term portability. They’re pricier but offer the best of both worlds if you move the machine between rooms or take it on weekend trips.

Volume and safety: how loud is too loud?

A safe volume level for a white noise machine is generally between 50 and 65 decibels, about the level of a quiet conversation or a gentle rainfall. At that range it masks most household sounds without risking hearing damage. Many machines can go louder, but prolonged exposure above 70 dB may strain your ears or affect hearing, especially for infants. For baby nurseries, place the machine at least a few feet away from the crib and never place it directly in the crib. Use the lowest effective volume that still masks disturbances. Some parents set the machine on a dresser across the room rather than on a bedside table. If you share a bed, find a machine with a speaker that can be aimed away from your partner, or choose one with a headphone jack for private listening. A few higher-end models even offer a pillow speaker accessory.

Extra features worth paying for?

Nightlights, smart connectivity, and multi-speaker setups can be nice but aren’t necessary for effective sound masking. A nightlight is handy in a nursery, but if it’s too bright it can disrupt sleep cycles. Look for a dimmable or amber-colored light if you need it. Bluetooth or Wi-Fi control via an app lets you adjust sound from your phone, set schedules, or even pair two machines for stereo masking. That’s useful for larger bedrooms or if you travel and want to control the unit remotely. However, these features add cost and potential connectivity headaches. Ultimately, the core job of a white noise machine is to produce a consistent, pleasant sound at the right volume. Don’t pay extra for bells and whistles you won’t use. A simple, well-reviewed model with good sound quality and reliable build is often the smartest choice.

Frequently asked questions

Can white noise damage my hearing?

Only if it’s played too loud for too long. Keeping the volume at or below 65 dB and following the manufacturer’s safe-distance guidelines, especially for babies, prevents any risk. Most modern machines have a maximum output that stays within safe limits.

How long should I run a white noise machine each night?

It’s fine to run it all night if you want consistent masking. Many people prefer a timer of 30–90 minutes because they fall asleep quickly and then enjoy silence. There’s no right answer; do what helps you sleep best.

Is white noise safe for babies and toddlers?

Yes, when used responsibly. Place the machine at least a few feet from the crib, set the volume to a calm level (under 65 dB), and avoid placing it directly in the crib or car seat. Many pediatric sleep specialists recommend it for soothing and blocking household noise.

Will a white noise machine block out a snoring partner?

It can help, but it depends on the loudness of the snore and your personal sensitivity. A quality machine with adjustable volume and a full-spectrum sound like pink noise may mask mild to moderate snoring. For very loud snoring, you might need a louder machine combined with earplugs.

Do white noise machines help with tinnitus?

They can provide relief by offering background sound that makes the ringing or buzzing less noticeable. Many tinnitus sufferers prefer pink noise or nature sounds over pure white noise, as the higher frequencies of white noise can sometimes feel irritating.

How do I clean and maintain my white noise machine?

Unplug it first, then use a soft, dry cloth to wipe the exterior. For mesh-covered speakers, gently brush or vacuum the grille to remove dust. Avoid getting moisture inside. If the machine uses a fan, check the air intake occasionally for lint buildup.

In shortFinding the right white noise machine comes down to your specific sleep environment and personal sound preferences. Prioritize adjustable volume, a broad selection of realistic noises, and dependable build quality. A simple, well-chosen model will serve you for years, making your bedroom a sanctuary of consistent calm.
